Observations

  1. Most software isn't built for you. It's built to keep you inside it.
  2. Everyone calls it a productivity tool. Nobody counts the hours it costs to run.
  3. At some point you stopped being the user. Now you're the subscription that funds their next feature.
  4. Removing a feature takes conviction, a meeting and someone willing to look boring. Most teams never find that person.
  5. The best tool is the one you stop thinking about.
  6. Before you automate anything, ask if it should exist at all.
  7. AI doesn't fix a broken process. It makes the broken parts happen faster.
  8. Five tools and a spreadsheet isn't a system.
  9. Every product worth respecting was shaped by what it refused to become.
  10. Simple is harder than complex. That's why so few things are.

Below, occasionally, I post what I'm thinking about.

2026.05.18On removing things

Most of the work is subtraction. You start with the obvious additions - a feature, a label, a reassurance - and then you spend the real time finding which of them were never holding anything up.

What remains after that is not a smaller version of the thing. It is the thing, finally legible.

2026.04.02Notes toward a calmer interface

A calm interface is one that has finished talking. It states what is true, offers what is needed and gets out of the way so you can think.

Every animation, badge and nudge should have to argue for its existence. Most of them lose the argument.
